Whipping it all together in a bowl isn't a good idea.

This idea literally bloomed two weeks into my school term when I first moved out having to live without a magical meal card. This meant having to do groceries, and planning what to do with those groceries.

Despite the fact I can look up videos and recipes online, it was difficult when I didn't even know what recipes actually existed to follow. Thus, I created Grumblr, an app that searches for recipes through entered ingredients. Surely there are apps out there that exist that do this, but this called for a good UX/UI practice.

The Marvel prototype.

The amalgamation of this Marvel prototype was done through a user-centered design approach. I created a persona named Juris, who will be the primary user of this app.

Juris Lay, the full-time store manager.
This persona was created to have a voice of the customer in mind while designing. (Although, in times of hunger during a side project such as this, empathy wasn't too difficult to muster).
How was Grumblr to help Juris?

Considering Juris' main goal is to find recipes faster and as well as conveniently know what to buy when she goes grocery shopping, it made practical sense to solve the problem with a mobile app due to its portability. Moreover, Juris has basic skills in using mobile apps—she just needs a more intuitive interface than the existing solutions.

Based on a task analysis I developed, Grumblr was to allow our persona Juris to not only look up recipes, but also save them and keep track of the dishes she would like to cook again.

Lo-fi to hi-fi, with Sketch and Marvel.

Accordingly, mockups were created in Sketch 3 to further show the flow and interaction design of how the app will fulfill the tasks based on the task analysis.

This shows that the primary screen contains the most important and foremost task Juris has to accomplish—searching for recipes based on ingredients she enters. Moreover, in real time it shows Juris how many recipes she can make depending on the ingredients she removes or adds.

The top header only shows two main options Juris would want to frequently visit: the home search screen, and her saved recipes.

For simplicity on how the app will run, it will gather data from the internet and open recipes much like how Facebook would open browser links within the app. However, this task is out of scope from our chosen task analysis.

According to the wireframes created, I proceeded to fully design the screens and prototype them in Marvel. I decided to use mustard and red as accents of the app as these colours are what I often associate with food. I had purple as the primary colour to help mute the warm colours, and add the subtlety that I am better than getting takeout from my favourite red and yellow fast-food chain representative.

A pat on the back, 8 months later.
